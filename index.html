IS JURASSIC PARK (1993) AN UNCONSCIOUS CONFESSION?

April 10th, 2:27am

Jurassic Park (1993); Twilight Zone: The Movie. Camera B (Master). July 23rd, 1982, 2:20am.

In 1982, veteran Hollywood actor Vic Morrow (age 53) and two illegally-hired child “extras,” Myca Le (age 7) and Renee Chen (age 6) were killed during the filming of a Vietnam war rescue scene on the set of Warner Bros’ Twilight Zone: The Movie (1983), when a Bell UH-1 “Huey” helicopter crashed on them.

Last known photo of Myca and Renee, July 22nd, 1982.

In the days after the accident, the Chen family filed a $200-million civil suit naming five corporations and nine individuals involved in the film’s production, reflecting early uncertainty about who was responsible.

Because the accident simultaneously involved an aircraft crash, a labour code violation, and a criminal matter, jurisdiction was split among the NTSB, Cal/OSHA, and the L.A. Sheriff’s Department, creating procedural confusion and weakening investigative authority. With no single agency clearly in charge, influential witnesses—particularly production and studio executives—had greater latitude to maneuver, delay cooperation, and exploit jurisdictional gaps, significantly limiting the police and prosecutors' leverage in the critical early stages. By the time the NTSB and Cal/OSHA completed their investigations at the end of 1982, only a single detective and a single D.A. remained to build the criminal case, severely disadvantaged against a formidable legal defense team funded by studios and insurers motivated to avoid a windfall in the pending civil trial. Although the prosecution nearly collapsed due to a lack of witness testimony—several witnesses later testified about serious threats of blacklisting—a breakthrough in early 1983 enabled the D.A. to successfully navigate the case through a Grand Jury hearing (June 1983), a preliminary hearing (February 1984), and an appeal (November 1984), until the defense had exhausted all legal options.

Finally, in a landmark trial in 1986-87, co-producer/writer/director, John Landis (Animal House, The Blues Brothers), along with two production subordinates, and two technicians, faced Involuntary Manslaughter charges for the deaths. Landis and his Associate Producer and Production Manager—who faced two counts specifically for the illegal hiring—conceded that they decided to hire the children without permits but said they only did so because of the late hour, not because they ever seriously believed the scene was dangerous as planned. The filmmakers were eventually acquitted, with the civil case settled during closing arguments for approximately $2-million dollars per family.

The verdict vindicated Landis and his Hollywood allies in their assertion that the accident had been unforeseeable:

  • “...an industrial accident, nothing more.” (Landis regular, Dan Aykroyd)

  • “...obviously a human catastrophe… not a criminal act.” (Universal Studios Production Executive, Sean Daniel)

  • “...a terrible tragedy […] terrible and horrible for everybody. But it was an accident. Which is eventually what the jury decided. (Twilight Zone Executive Producer, Frank Marshall)

And after that, it all pretty much went away.

But, in truth, the case was fraught and controversial.

Despite[a] some one-hundred witnesses to the accident, mostly crew, the set had been cleared by the time investigators arrived. Myriad evidence went missing, including one of the shot’s two audio recordings; the on-set photographer’s negatives; all of the producer’s “Making Of” documentary footage, except for a single shot of a technical rehearsal; several “thumbnail sketches”; and all graphic and visual materials relating to the film’s production, except for a single bird’s eye diagram of the set known as “the spotting plan,” which “by some ungodly coincidence” included a depiction of a helicopter which had already crashed. The defence resisted turning over even this lone, well-corroborated image to the NTSB until the matter was escalated to a federal court.

As well—most significantly, for our purposes—a man who turned out to be one of the case’s key witnesses—the movie’s Executive Producer, Frank Marshall (Raiders of the Lost Ark, Poltergeist)—managed to avoid speaking with investigators throughout the case’s five years. Marshall was informed about Landis’s intentions by a casting agent over a month in advance; whose signature appears on the cheque used to pay the children for their first night of work; whose accountant was reported by a production assistant to have provided her an envelope full of cash on the day of the second night; who was on-set both nights the children were there, including during earlier shots with the children which also constituted legal violations; who ordered the set cleared; and who was documented by Landis’s secretary as raiding Landis’s office for production materials the morning after the accident. Landis even testified that Marshall explicitly approved the illegal hiring, a narrative maintained by Landis’s two subordinates. This is especially significant since Marshall was an executive officer at both Twilight Zone Productions, and the payroll company contracted by Warner Bros to act as a watchdog on the production’s spending, which, legally-speaking, made him a major person of interest. Domestic and international subpoenas were issued for Marshall’s testimony, but Marshall spent most of the next four years overseas and out of reach. By the time Marshall finally surfaced, as the result of an Interpol “Stop and Hold” order, the prosecution and defense had already rested their cases, and his testimony was no longer of any use.

Frank Marshall, Photographed in France, 1985.

The Twilight Zone tragedy has been analyzed in three excellent books—Special Effects (LaBrecque, 1988); Outrageous Conduct (Farber and Green, 1988); and Fly by Night (Chain, 2022)—all written by journalists who attended the trial. The absence of Marshall’s testimony is significant within the design and enigma of the case because it precluded Marshall’s being asked even basic questions about the degree of involvement of co-producer, Steven Spielberg.

Twilight Zone: the Movie was an anthology film, featuring four segments by four different directors, including Spielberg, with Landis’s having been the first to go to production.

Spielberg (age 36) discovered the project during an invitation to browse Warner Bros’ development slate in January of 1982; he brought it to Landis in March, proposing a collaboration, knowing Landis also loved the intellectual property. Although the two first met at Martha’s Vineyard in 1974, when Landis was considered as a screenwriter for what would become Close Encounters, they only became friendly in 1978, after Spielberg reached out to Landis following the success of Animal House. As detailed by Chain (Ch. 6) the two exhibited behaviour reflective of a passive-aggressive friendship, with Landis perceiving that Spielberg had used some of his ideas for Close Encounters without compensation at a time in his career when he was desperate for an opportunity. In her notorious Hollywood tell-all, Julia Phillips claimed: “Steven hated him, although Michael and I always wondered if he felt threatened, Landis being so child-prodigy and all, Steven pretty much feeling he had the corner on that arena. There’s always someone younger than you.”

According to accounts of the early stages of production, Spielberg was heavily invested in the development of the film, both as a whole, and with regards to his own originally-planned sequence. Prior to the accident, the Twilight Zone segments were going to be more continuous and less discrete than they ended up becoming, with recurring characters, seamless segues, and winks to earlier sequences, according to Joe Dante (Gremlins), the director of the movie’s third sequence. George Miller (Mad Max), the director of the fourth sequence, said that when he was brought on the project in May, he met with Spielberg, Marshall, and Kennedy, indicating that the project was on everyone’s radar.

As well, by 1982, Spielberg was in a state of self-described filmmaking mania.

60 Minutes Australia, 1982:

…And all of a sudden I realized that I was a non-entity. I was existing only on celluloid, and I was losing my own identification with Steven Spielberg. And I was forming sprocket holes down both sides of my face and frame lines across my body. And I was starting to move at about eight-frames-a-second, which is too fast to be moving, in movie jargon.

Spielberg was already embroiled in a PR controversy over his alleged backseat directing of Tobe Hooper’s Poltergeist, when Spielberg’s title was Executive Producer.

The DGA fined Spielberg $15,000 for “denigrating the role of the director” by way of the size of the names in the credits, and ordered MGM to redo trailers already running in New York and L.A., and to apologize to Hooper publicly in three trade magazines. It’s unlikely the studio would have released the trailer without Spielberg’s approval. Spielberg took out his own two ads (see adjacent). All of this followed persistent reports (continuing through at least May 1982) that Spielberg had been taking the reins (McBride 336-40).

On June 2nd, 1982, Spielberg published a letter in two trade magazines apologizing for the perception that he was overbearing. Note the typist’s initials. Spielberg wrote Poltergeist, and had originally wanted to direct it himself, but because of the rush to get E.T. to production, Spielberg would be unable to direct Poltergeist due to DGA policy.

Marshall never spoke on the record about the incident except once, in 1990, while promoting his directorial debut, Amblin’s Arachnaphobia.

Asked to comment on those matters during an interview for “Arachnophobia,” Marshall called the stories about his alleged evasion of subpoenas “all hype. There’s no truth to it at all.” …

“Actually I was here (Los Angeles) a long time, two years, and nobody ever talked to me,” Marshall said. “Then we went off to England and made ‘Empire of the Sun,’ ‘Roger Rabbit’ and ‘Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade.’ So I lived in London, and the time most of the trial was going on, we were in China (filming ‘Empire of the Sun’).

“So, I was inaccessible. And I didn’t really feel that I had anything to add that wasn’t already out there, than what had already been revealed.” (LA Times)

The article also notes, however:

While Marshall himself was not indicted in the case, deputy district attorney Gilbert Garcetti told reporters after the trial that his office made “every effort possible to subpoena Frank Marshall but he was able to get out of our hands.”

The problem presented by Marshall’s actions, in terms of resolving the facts of the accident, is the question of what motivated it. Marshall (age 36) had recently co-founded Amblin Entertainment, Ltd., with his girlfriend, Kathleen Kennedy (age 27), and their boss and friend, Steven Spielberg. When they met, Spielberg’s career was at a turning point. His last three movies, Jaws (1975), Close Encounters of the Third Kind (1977), and 1941 (1979), had all gone way over time and budget; this had been fine, until Spielberg’s first major bid at comedy, 1941, flopped both critically and commercially. Spielberg consciously shifted gears starting with Raiders of the Lost Ark, with newly disciplined production procedures, lead by Marshall—whom Spielberg remembered favorably from a passing encounter on the set of a Peter Bogdanovich movie in 1974—and Kennedy, who had only been a Production Assistant on 1941, but had impressed Spielberg with her creative story suggestions, and took it upon herself to organize his notes. Not only was Raiders a massive success, but Poltergeist and E.T. would also be released within the same year-long period. Amblin had plans to produce swaths of children’s and family programming throughout the 1980s.

Beyond negligently facilitating the hiring unwittingly despite being warned, Marshall is implicated in consciously facilitating the hiring, by the actions of Twilight Zone production accountant, Bonne Radford. Radford worked on the Warner Bros lot, in close contact with Marshall; while Landis and his group worked out of an assembly of trailers on the Universal Studios lot. Amblin would only move their offices from the Warner Bros lot to its final location on the Universal lot in 1984. The original office bungalow was documented by a Japanese TV crew in November/December 1982.

Radford, again, is reported to have asked a Warner Bros accountant how to go about hiring two children without permits for the Landis group and then, subsequently, unrelatedly, asking him to co-sign a $2,000 cheque for a personal cash advance to the Production’s Associate Producer. She is also reported to have given a Production Assistant an envelope full of cash from an address on the Warner Bros lot the day before the accident. If these witnesses are to be believed, then on whose authority was Radford facilitating this illegal act? It’s unlikely she would have done so on behalf of the Associate Producer or Landis. And if Marshall—as stands to reason—told her to do it, then who told Marshall? Why would Marshall go out on a limb to facilitate John Landis’s being able to film two children at night for a special effects scene?

Photo of children from night before the accident, July 21st, 1982 (the shoot’s originally intended wrap date, and scheduled “family day.”)

Marshall was present the originally-scheduled last night of shooting (July 21st/22nd), when the two children (6-and-7-years-old) were kept up until 5:30am filming some close-up shots. Sometime earlier—around 4:00am, according to Landis—the 1st AD informed him it would not be possible to complete the three major special effects setups and various pick-ups they still had left on the schedule to shoot. Landis said he confirmed it with Marshall. It was only then as they were wrapping up that the Associate Producer, as he was paying the parents out in cash, asked them if they could come back the next night. He said that they would finish up “first thing,” within an hour or an hour and a half.

That evening, Marshall was on-set at 9:30pm to witness the first of the two shots involving the children that night.

2 of 3 camera angles recovered from the 9:30pm shot, at which time Marshall was present and, according to Chain, so was Kennedy (Chain Ch. 47). If you were a producer of childrens’ and family films, and you watched this scene, would you be alarmed? Twice (before the shot and during) Morrow was observed by those present to drop (before/run-through) Myca, and (during) Renee into the water. Landis testified that he asked the children whether or not they could swim, and that “one said yes, and the other said no.

Marshall was also on-set for the following shot (first in the chronology of the story), while the families were being sequestered in a trailer about a mile away, near the entrance of the park, asked to please wait around for the last shot, where the children would finally be carried all the way across the river, as they were told.

The 11:30pm shot, during which the pilot (a Vietnam veteran inexperienced with movie work) was startled by an unexpected blast, got spooked, and turned and left the scene, nearly missing another blast that nearly torched the crew inside, which was a topic of conversation at the subsequent crew “lunch.”

The significance of Marshall’s non-cooperation was pointed out explicitly by Landis’s lawyer in late 1985, in a final bid to get the office counts disposed of.

The issue involved in the "Twilight Zone" case as charged by your office is whether the defendants knew about a serious danger on the set and chose to disregard that danger. Simple interviews with Spielberg, Marshall, and Kennedy would reveal that these highly placed and acknowledged experts knew that a scene was being shot in their own movie in which actors were being used in a scene with a helicopter and special effects. These participants knew the elements of the scene and would have stopped the production had they ever thought that special effects could not be safely used with actors and helicopters. For Kesselman to have pursued the conspiracy to employ minors without a permit would have resulted in the destruction of his manslaughter case against the five targeted defendants.

Frank Marshall was on the set on the night of the accident as well as on the previous night. Marshall saw the children present and saw them in the 9:30 shot in which they were held by Vic Morrow in a scene with a helicopter and special effects. Marshall was present the whole evening and observed the 11:30 sequence involving Morrow, the 2:00 rehearsal as well as the scene in which the accident occurred. Marshall was the producer of the "Twilight Zone" and participated at meetings in which the hiring of the children was discussed and agreed upon. Since the statute of limitations has now expired, he has no fifth amendment right and if asked will hopefully tell the truth. But what must be emphasized again is the fact that his participation in the hiring of the children without permits was viewed as a "technical" violation that in no way meant that anyone was in danger.

Marshall and Kennedy could verify that Kennedy called the Department of Labor to determine whether permits could be obtained for minors after hours. It was only after her call received a negative response, that the decision was made to employ the children without permits. Again, Ms. Kennedy had no belief that there was any danger even though she was intimately acquainted with the nature of the shot and the use of the children. In fact, she was on the set the prior night when the scenes filmed on the fatal night were scheduled to be shot.

With respect to Mr. Spielberg, Marshall and Kennedy will be able to state whether he knew that children were to be employed without permits. It could be reasonably inferred that given the close relationship between Spielberg, Marshall and Kennedy and Spielberg’s close supervision of his own movies, that it would be almost inconceivable that Marshall and Kennedy would not tell Spielberg that children were to be employed without permits on Spielberg’s own movie.

Your office's statement that there was an exhaustive investigation is not only false with respect to Marshall, but is also false with respect to Spielberg. Spielberg was able to deflect the investigation away from himself by simply submitting a letter stating that he was not on the set the night of the accident. His full and complete knowledge and approval of what was to take place must be assumed and never has been disputed.

What other major witness could avoid questioning by signing a piece of paper? Your office never asked Spielberg a single question about the accident or the hiring of the children. Your claim that Spielberg was "unavailable" is simply false.

Associate Producer Kathleen Kennedy (Spielberg segment) herself made the blind call to the Department of Labor. It was only when she received a negative reply to a question of getting permits for night work that the decision was made to employ the children without permits. Kennedy was on the set the night before the accident when the children were present and the special effects were being set up while the helicopter rehearsed. Why has she not been interviewed?

Spielberg (or his representatives) gave four statements directed to the matter over the years.

First, in response to the Chen family civil lawsuit filed on August 2nd, 1982: “Spielberg is informed and believes and thereon alleges that at the time and place of the events complained of, plaintiffs and each of them were not exercising ordinary care, caution or prudence to prevent the injuries sustained by them or by decedent and that, therefore, the injuries alleged were proximately caused by the negligence or comparative negligence of the plaintiffs."

Second, in response to the NTSB interview of a special effects truck driver on November 5th, 1982:

Mr. Pitman [sic] stated that following the crash scene, he had assisted Mr. Landis and one of the children's parents in obtaining a vehicle** to leave the set, and likewise, had secured a vehicle for STEVEN SPIELBERG to leave the set. Following this he left the set, driving the 10-ton powder truck followed by Ted, who was driving the special effects pickup truck. He stated that both of them drove directly to the Burbank Studios. He stated that it was the following day when he and members of the special effects crew met at the Burbank Studios and off-loaded material.

But Pittman’s testimony was dubious, as he’d already admitted to being drunk on set. So of course he must  be lying.

Spielberg replied to the NTSB’s inquiry on the matter by way of a signed affidavit:

In response to your request, I was never at the Indian Dunes location of Twilight Zone on the night of the accident or at any other time. I declare under penalty of perjury that the foregoing is true and correct, executed at Los Angeles, California, this first day of December, 1982.

Pittman admitted he’d probably had Spielberg confused with Marshall.

Third, a short comment to Los Angeles Times Reporter Dale Pollock:

This has been the most interesting year of my film career… It has mixed the best, the success of E.T., with the worst, the Twilight Zone tragedy. A mixture of ecstasy and grief. It's made me grow up a little more. The accident cast a pall on all 150 people who worked on this production. We are still just sick to the center of our souls. I don't know anybody who it hasn't affected.

A movie is a fantasy—it's light and shadow flickering on a screen. No movie is worth dying for. I think people are standing up much more now than ever before to producers and directors who ask too much. If something isn't safe, it's the right and responsibility of every actor or crew member to yell, 'Cut!'" (Spielberg Philosophical Over E.T. Defeat, April 13, 1983)

And fourth, a variation on his previous year’s Oscar comment, to the same reporter:

It was tough on all of us, all 390 people involved in making the movie, and it continues to be. (Spielberg Gets Place to Settle Down, May 21, 1984)

The number had been adjusted to include the entire production (LaBrecque).

When reporter Sandy Kenyon brought up the accident during a 1985 CNN interview for The Color Purple, Spielberg waved his hands and asked that the camera be turned off (Farber & Green).

Suffice to say, Marshall had his own reasons for evading investigators. The question of whether Marshall knew the children were going to be used not only for the 9:30pm shot, but the 2:20am shot as well is unclear. The Locations Manager, Richard Vane, who had worked with Marshall on Poltergeist, testified once, at the Grand Jury, that during the final shot Marshall, who was standing beside him, turned to say, “those are dolls he’s picking up, aren’t they?” But Vane was the highest ranking member of the production to admit to participating in a conspiracy to conceal the presence of the children from a particular Fire Safety Officer known to be trained as an on-set Teacher and Child Welfare Worker, up to and including the 2:20am shot.

Spielberg never distanced himself from Marshall despite the negligence or subpoenas. Not only Marshall, but Vane and Radford also continued to flourish within the Amblin organization, with Vane graduating to Producer with Harry and the Hendersons in 1987, and Radford graduating to Producer with We’re Back! A Dinosaur’s Story in 1994.

The two children were only added to Landis’s segment with the third draft of the script, dated June 13th, 1982. In the story, Morrow’s character—an embittered bigot—delivers a tirade to his friends in a bar, and then steps out into Nazi-occupied France. Shortly, he is mistaken for Jewish and chased by the officers, before time-jumping again to the deep south, where he continues to be chased, but now by the KKK. Next, he finds himself in Vietnam, where he finds himself set upon—not by Vietnamese soldiers, who pass by his hiding spot—but by U.S. infantry. He is finally buffeted back to the Nazi timeline, where he is hauled to a half-filled freight car headed for the death camps. He reaches out of the slats to his friends, whom he can now see stumbling out of the bar, but they can’t hear his cries.

Landis testified that the recurring complaint he received from the Warner Brothers Executive class, as filtered to him through Director of Creative Lucy Fisher, was that the story was just too darn cynical.

Landis complained that he wrote it in the spirit of the original series, which, as we all recall, was all about morals this, and morals that.

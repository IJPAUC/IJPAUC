1. “8:00 or 8:30pm”

In Landis’s testimony (Feb 1987), he reported that on approx. Friday, July 9th, 1982, a second meeting between Marshall, himself, Folsey, and Allingham took place at Landis’s offices on the Universal Studios lot. He said that the first meeting—approx. Tuesday, July 6th, 1982—had hinged on Marshall’s question as to whether it would be possible to get waivers for the young children to work past 6:30pm, which Marshall knew to be the rule. The defendants say Marshall said he would have his colleague, Kathleen Kennedy, call the Labor Board to find out.

During the second meeting, the defendants say Marshall informed them that waivers could only be gotten until “8:00 or 8:30pm.” James Neal introduced this distinction for the first time during his opening statements for the criminal trial in September 1986.

According to Chain, Marshall told them to “forget about” the prospect of getting waivers for children younger than 8-years-old to film at night.

The defendants proceeded with the illegal hiring, under the impression that Marshall had endorsed their plan, and Marshall has never either confirmed or denied their account.

But if the defendants’ account of the “8:00 or 8:30pm” detail is correct, the question is raised of where Marshall came up with that number.

Colleen Logan, the Area Administrator for the Division of Labor Standards Enforcement, testified that, upon request, the Department generally grants an extension until exactly 10:00pm (Cross Feb 87; Closing Arguments, May 87).

The significance of this discrepancy—as regards the factors influencing the 2:20am shot—is that it raises the question of why Marshall would report the incorrect (earlier) timeframe to the defendants?

Marshall and/or his liaison could’ve simply received or remembered the wrong information.

But is it possible that Marshall—with his manifest experience on producing sci-fi adventure films with children—already knew that waivers were unlikely to be granted not only because of the timeframe but because of their proximity to major special effects?

More likely, Marshall knew, as the defendants all basically knew, it was illegal because of the proximity to special effects (even if they believed it wasn’t seriously dangerous). The defendants, however, did have a more plausible claim (than Marshall) to being naive about the seriousness of the crime they were committing. Marshall had more deniability.

This would make any phone call to any Labor authority moot and, obviously, a bad idea with regards to drawing unnecessary attention to the production.

But, then, if Marshall knew it wouldn’t be allowed and reported an approximation (“8:00 or 8:30”), why (in doing so) did he misrepresent it to the defendants as a matter of the hour, and not proximity to special effects (in effect normalizing to the defendants the relevant, unstated, violation)?

And why, anyway, would Marshall facilitate a hiring he knew to be illegal rather than saying, “get inventive; use dummies”?

Was he that willing to go to bat for the creative vision of a director, John Landis, whom he knew to be a complete liability?

Was getting the children into the scene that important?

Was there some reason they needed a close-up of a girl holding a doll?

5. Scene 32 & 34 (Shed pick-ups)

The production intended to get a quick pair of pick-up shots after the 2:20am shot, namely Scenes 32 & 34 (33 having already been filmed).

32 ASIAN RIVER - NIGHT
The helicopter spots them and guns blazing, heads toward the river. As Bill carrying the kids desperately makes his way through the water, the helicopter makes several passes strafing the area. Bill makes it to shore and sees a wooden shed. Making a super-human attempt he manages to get all three of them inside. He stacks the wood inside against the door, then huddles with the kids in the corner.

BILL
(breathless)
Don’t worry you guys. I won’t let them hurt you.

  1. EXT. SHED - THE WOODS - NIGHT

The shed is now in the pine woods. Dogs barking, torches burning.

The-picks-ups arrive. The Klan surrounds the small shed.

K.K.K. #1
We got us a n***** in the wood pile!

K.K.K. #3
Let’s burn him!

K.K.K. #2
No, I want him alive. His black ass is mine.

Grabbing axes they start chopping down the door.

34 INT. SHED - NIGHT
The door begins to splinter under the axes. The door shutters and the Nazi soldiers grab Bill and pull him out.

We know this because the Extras playing the two Nazi soldiers were in attendance for the accident.

These scenes are significant for providing plausible deniability to Dick Vane, the locations manager, who was the highest ranking member of the production to admit to participating in a conspiracy to conceal the children from a fire safety officer and welfare worker. He was able at the Grand Jury hearing to both (1) admit he was actively participating in the conspiracy at the time of the accident (2:20am); and (2) claim that he, and Frank Marshall—standing beside him—didn’t believe the children were going to be used in the shot. Vane was able to say that he believed the concealment plan was ongoing for the sake of the last two “shed” pick-up shots (Scene 32 & 34).

But was it true?

Tice told the Grand Jury that Vane checked on his whereabouts three times in the lead-up to the 2:20am shot specifically.

Did someone have conversations with you on Thursday that in retrospect you now think were somewhat out of the ordinary or order?

“Yes, Richard Vane wanted to know where I Was going to be during the filming of the 2:30 scene… he asked me that on three occasions. I think it was unusual. I did not think much of it at the time, but afterwards I thought it was quite unusual. And the reason for it is because the fire deputy had— we had six firemen there, and we were coordinating the fire efforts, representing the county fire department. And it was quite unusual for him to insist on knowing where I was going to be.”

6. "So I got him a car."

In response to an inquiry from the NTSB, following sworn testimony by an unreliable witness (an SFX truck driver, Carl Pittman, who’d admitted to being drunk among other liabilities) that Spielberg had been on set and asked him to call his limousine in the aftermath of the accident (LaBrecque 76; only LaBrecque specifies limousine rather than car, and he doesn’t explain).

Two other items of trivia/hearsay, from Chain:

{i} On July 15th, the Thursday night that Landis auditioned the Hua family at the Franklin Reservoir, Folsey, Jr. “created a sensation on the rundown street by pulling up in a limo.” (Ch. 24) [Like a scene out of Blank Check.]

{ii} Warner Bros. studio heard a rumour that Spielberg interviewed the children on the Twilight Zone set a week before the crash. (Ch. 26)
